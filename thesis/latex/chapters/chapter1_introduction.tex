\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Positioning} \label{ch:introduction_section:positioning}
We live in a world where automation is being adopted at a rapidly increasing pace.
Luggage is sorted and autonomously transported across airports, autonomous vacuum cleaners are used daily, medicines are produced automatically with extreme cleanliness, etc.
This trend also applies to robotics, with robots becoming increasingly prevalent in factories and warehouses to improve efficiency, quality, and safety.

To operate properly, these robots require sophisticated systems, which are increasingly based on \gls{ai}.
More specifically, \gls{cv} is a subdomain of \gls{ai} that enables computers to interpret and make decisions based on visual data from cameras.
This technology can be used to detect and localize objects, enabling the robot to process them.
Additionally, more general \gls{ai} can be used to plan the robot's actions and control its actuators to perform desired tasks.
Furthermore, systems can be trained to monitor processes, detect defects in finished products, or predict maintenance needs.
The use of \gls{ai} thus not only brings greater efficiency and quality gains but also enables robots to execute much more complex tasks.

These real-world scenarios require \gls{ai} systems to be highly robust and reliable.
However, \gls{ai} systems often struggle when faced with new or unknown situations, leading to unreliable predictions.
Therefore, systems should be able to express uncertainty about their predictions.
This capability enables safety mechanisms to prevent the robot from taking actions based on uncertain predictions.
Uncertainty-aware \gls{ai} models achieve this by outputting both a prediction and a corresponding uncertainty score.

\section{Problem statement} \label{ch:introduction_section:problem-statement}
While an \gls{ai} system offers many advantages in a factory setting, it also introduces new challenges that must be addressed.
One of the issues with these systems is that they will always provide an answer, even when they are unsure of it.
Consequently, if the system is uncertain about its output, it will not inform the user and will continue operating normally.
This can sometimes be beneficial, as the system might still make the correct decision, thereby increasing efficiency compared to a human worker who would need to pause and assess the new situation.
However, it is more likely to be detrimental, as incorrect decisions can lead to product defects, damage to the robot or its environment, or harm to nearby humans.

A system might be unsure about its output if it needs to infer information from data that was not (properly) represented during training.
Most training data is gathered in controlled environments where all conditions are optimal.
However, the system's performance can significantly worsen in real-world scenarios that differ from the training environment.
These differences can include varying lighting conditions due to shadows or reflections, camera lens distortion from dust, or sudden changes in illumination.
Other unexpected events, such as a human operator placing the wrong object or the sudden movement of an already placed object, can also disrupt the \gls{ai} system.

\section{Objectives} \label{ch:introduction_section:objectives}
This thesis aims to adress these issues by developing and evaluating uncertainty-aware regression models which infer the center ($x$ and $y$ coordinates) of an object. 
This so a robot arm can grasp them, if the uncertainty is low, otherwise avoid taking actions to keep the environment safe. 
Our focus is on making these models work in challenging real-world conditions.

The primary research question is:
How can we develop uncertainty-aware object localization models for a robot arm, that can effectively identify and communicate uncertainty in their predictions, particularly in challenging real-world conditions?

This overarching question is further explored through the following sub-questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What uncertainty aware (object localization) models already exist, and how do they perform in real-world conditions?
    \item How can we effectively quantify and communicate uncertainty in object localization predictions to ensure safe robot operation?
\end{itemize}

To address these questions, the following objectives are set:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Conduct a comprehensive literature review on uncertainty and existing uncertainty-aware models with an emphesis on object localization.
    \item Create a dataset where the center of an object ($x$ and $y$ coordinate) is annotated. This in both normal and challenging real-world conditions.
    \item Develop, implement and evaluate uncertainty-aware models to predict the center of an object along with an uncertainty score.
    \item Design and implement safety mechanisms that utilize the uncertainty information to prevent the robot from taking actions based on uncertain predictions.
\end{itemize}
